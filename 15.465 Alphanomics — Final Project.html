A two-stage empirical framework for asset pricing. Estimate which firm characteristics historically predicted monthly stock returns; then translate those estimates into a forced Buy / Neutral / Sell call across any set of public companies in any sector.

01.

The project, in five movements.

Workflow
i.

Pre-register the universe

Three or more publicly traded firms in the same business sector and same listing country. The initial assignment uses CEG, NEE, and VST — three U.S. power and utilities names. The platform supports any sector with up to ten companies.

ii.

Univariate Fama-MacBeth regressions

For each candidate signal, run sixty monthly cross-sectional regressions of returns on that signal alone. Average the sixty slopes; compute the Fama-MacBeth standard error from the time-series of slopes. Each signal's predictive power, in isolation.

iii.

Multivariate Fama-MacBeth regressions

Same procedure, all signals entered simultaneously. This isolates each signal's marginal contribution after controlling for the others. Significant survivors at |t| > 1.645 form the basis of the live recommendation.

iv.

Implied annualized Sharpe ratios

Translate each signal's t-statistic into a risk-adjusted return: SR = (t / √T) × √12. Ranks the signals by economic magnitude rather than statistical detection.

v.

Forced recommendation

For each surviving signal, rank the live companies high-to-low. Multiply each rank by the historical Fama-MacBeth coefficient. Sum. The highest composite is Buy, the lowest is Sell, the middle is Neutral.

MODULE · 03 · METHODOLOGY

The methodology.

From a panel of firm-month observations to a single Buy/Neutral/Sell decision. The math is standard Fama-MacBeth. The discipline is in not letting the answer wander beyond what the t-statistics support.

03.1

Fama-MacBeth in two passes.

Foundations
Step 1 — monthly cross-sections.
For each month t in [1, T]:
   reti,t = αt + βt · signali,t + εi,t Save βt. After T months, we have a time-series of T monthly slopes for each signal.
Step 2 — time-series of slopes.
avg_β = mean(βt)
SE = std(βt) / √T
t-stat = avg_β / SE Significance test: |t| > 1.645 corresponds to the 10% level. The platform applies this threshold by default.
Implied annualized Sharpe ratio.
SR = (t-stat / √T) × √12 Translates statistical detection into an investable risk-adjusted return. Sharpes > ±2 are economically meaningful.
03.2

From regression to recommendation.

The composite score
A.
Filter to surviving signals

Keep only signals with |t-stat| > 1.645 in the multivariate regression.

B.
Rank companies cross-sectionally on each signal

Highest raw value receives a rank of 1.0; lowest receives 0.0; intermediate firms are evenly spaced. We rank rather than multiply raw values, because the Fama-MacBeth coefficients were estimated on standardized signals; raw multiplication would mix scales.

C.
Weight each rank by the historical FM coefficient

This automatically penalizes high rankings on negative-signed signals (volatility, xfin, inssell) and rewards high rankings on positive ones. Coefficient magnitude determines the relative importance of each signal — momentum at +0.0197 carries roughly five times the weight of book-to-market at +0.0039.

D.
Sum across signals → composite α

One scalar per firm:

composite_α(firm) = Σsignals   rankfirm,signal × βsignal,FM
E.
Forced ranking

Highest composite → Buy. Lowest composite → Sell. Everything in between → Neutral. The forced split is intentional — the project asks for a relative-value call across a fixed universe, not a market-wide screen.

03.3

Written analysis · Part 1.

Rubric responses

Univariate · Which signals appear to have the strongest relationship with returns?

Based on t-statistics and average coefficients, momentum (t = 9.63), earnings surprise (t = 7.85), and SUE (t = 7.50) show the strongest positive relationships with cross-sectional returns. On the inverse side, insider selling (t = −7.11) and external financing (t = −6.54) demonstrate the strongest negative univariate relationships. All five exceed the |t| > 1.645 threshold by a wide margin.

Univariate · How stable are the coefficient estimates over time?

The top performers are highly stable. Momentum, SUE, and earnings surprise yield positive coefficients in 85% to 90% of the sixty observed months, indicating consistent predictive power. Insider selling and external financing are conversely negative in 80% to 87% of months. The low standard error implied by the high t-statistics shows no extreme structural breaks for these factors. Less consistent signals — book-to-market (58% positive months) and F-Score (55%) — show flatter coefficient series and weaker time-series significance, which previews their attenuated multivariate role.

Univariate · Why do these signals 'work'?

Momentum and earnings surprise (PEAD). Both reflect investor underreaction to new information — a behavioral bias documented since Bernard & Thomas (1989). When firms beat consensus, the market price does not immediately and fully incorporate the surprise; the residual drift produces predictable monthly returns over subsequent months.

Insider selling. Reflects information asymmetry. Corporate insiders observe fundamentals at a higher frequency and resolution than the market and time their personal trades on perceived overvaluation. The cross-sectional signal aggregates this private information.

External financing. Combines two effects: (i) market-timing — managers issue equity when they perceive the stock is overvalued, and (ii) investment-policy — heavy capital raising signals overinvestment that earns sub-economic returns. Both produce subsequent return underperformance.

Volatility. The "low-volatility anomaly" — high-volatility stocks earn lower risk-adjusted returns than CAPM predicts. Likely combines lottery-preference behavior (investors overpay for high-vol upside) and leverage-constraint frictions (institutional investors lever up low-vol names instead of buying high-vol ones).

Gross profitability. A risk-adjusted compensation effect documented by Novy-Marx — high gross-profitability firms have higher expected returns even after controlling for value and size. Often interpreted as a quality premium that the market under-rewards.

Multivariate · Which variables remain significant?

At the |t| > 1.645 threshold, nine of ten signals survive in the multivariate regression: momentum, gross profitability, earnings surprise, SUE, accruals, book-to-market on the positive side; volatility, external financing, and insider selling on the negative side. Only F-Score falls below the cutoff (t = 1.00). This is consistent with F-Score being a composite signal — its information is already captured by its component pieces (gross profitability, accruals, external financing) once those enter the regression directly.

Multivariate · How do coefficients change when controlling for other signals?

For most signals, coefficients and t-statistics attenuate modestly relative to the univariate run. The largest attenuation appears for the earnings-related signals: SUE drops from t = 7.50 to t = 4.28, and earnings surprise from t = 7.85 to t = 4.75 — both signals share substantial cross-sectional information, so each captures the other's marginal effect. Momentum, in contrast, is essentially unchanged (t = 9.63 univariate → t = 9.87 multivariate), confirming that momentum carries information orthogonal to the fundamental signals. Insider selling and external financing are similarly stable, suggesting their information content is largely independent of price-based or earnings-based signals.

Multivariate · What does the average R² tell us?

The multivariate average R² is approximately 0.22, meaning the ten-signal model collectively explains roughly 22% of the cross-sectional variance in monthly stock returns in an average month. This is high for cross-sectional asset-pricing regressions — individual stock returns are dominated by idiosyncratic noise, and 2% to 5% R² is common in published work. The 22% figure reflects the standardized signal construction in the simulated panel, which removes some of the noise present in raw real-world data.

Multivariate · Interpret coefficient magnitudes in economic terms

Because all signals are standardized (mean = 0, σ = 1) but returns are not, each coefficient is the expected change in monthly stock return for a one-standard-deviation increase in the signal, holding the others constant. For momentum (β = +0.0197), a firm with momentum one σ above the cross-sectional mean is expected to earn ~1.97% higher monthly return than an average firm. For insider selling (β = −0.0259), a firm with insider selling one σ above the mean is expected to earn ~2.59% lower monthly return. Annualized (× 12), these correspond to spreads of roughly 24 percentage points for momentum and 31 percentage points for insider selling — economically very large, which is why the implied Sharpe ratios are so high.

Sharpe · Which four signals have the strongest performance?

By absolute implied annualized Sharpe ratio, the four strongest signals are momentum (+4.41), gross profitability (+3.27), insider selling (−3.02), and external financing (−2.80). Two are positive momentum-style behavioral signals; two are negative information / market-timing signals. The mix is informative: real alpha appears to come from combining behavioral underreaction (momentum, profitability) with information asymmetry (insider trading, equity issuance) — distinct sources that should diversify in a portfolio context.

Sharpe · How might these results inform investment strategy design?

Three implications. First, signal weighting should follow Sharpe rather than t-stat alone — momentum at SR 4.4 deserves roughly twice the weight of earnings surprise at SR 2.1, even though both are highly significant. Second, a long-short construction is preferred over long-only, because four of the strongest signals are negative-sided (insider selling, external financing, volatility, F-score-related). Going short on the negative tail captures alpha that a long-only fund cannot access. Third, signal diversification matters: combining one behavioral signal (momentum) with one corporate-action signal (insider selling) should reduce portfolio variance versus stacking similar signals (momentum + earnings surprise + SUE all behavioral underreaction).

03.4

Per-company narrative.

Part 2B · why each call

NextEra Energy (NEE) — BUY

NEE earns the highest composite α score (+0.01246) and the Buy designation. The score is driven primarily by NEE's top rank (1.0) on two of the largest positive-coefficient signals: price momentum (β = +0.0197) and book-to-market (β = +0.0039). NEE's 12-month price momentum of 0.421 is the strongest among the three names — material exposure to the renewable energy build-out and steady regulated-utility cash flows have driven price appreciation. Its book-to-market ratio of 0.247, while low in absolute terms, is the highest of the three, signaling relatively less premium pricing versus peers. NEE also benefits from moderate earnings surprise and the lowest stock volatility (0.265) of the three, which reduces the penalty from the negative volatility coefficient. The model does penalize NEE for ranking highest on external financing (positive xfin reflects its capital-intensive renewable project pipeline funded through debt and equity issuance), but the magnitude of momentum and value premium more than offsets this drag.

Vistra Corp (VST) — NEUTRAL

VST occupies the middle of the forced distribution at composite α = +0.00593. Its profile is bifurcated. On the positive side, VST has the lowest insider selling of the three firms — meaning it receives effectively zero penalty from the most heavily-weighted negative coefficient (β = −0.0259). It also has middle ranks on momentum (0.360, second-best) and gross profitability (0.167, strong). On the negative side, VST suffered a Q1 EPS miss, giving it the lowest rank (0.0) on both earnings surprise and SUE — signals which the model penalizes due to the post-earnings-announcement drift documented in Part 1. The net result is a slightly positive but unexceptional composite, consistent with a hold rather than active long or short.

Constellation Energy (CEG) — SELL

CEG receives the lowest composite α (−0.00560) and the forced Sell. The scoring illustrates the value of a multi-signal cross-sectional approach. Despite having the strongest raw earnings surprise of the three — Q1 2024 saw CEG report EPS that materially exceeded consensus — CEG ranks last across several other heavily-weighted dimensions. It has the lowest 12-month momentum (0.239) of the three; the highest stock volatility (0.514), penalized by the volatility coefficient; the highest insider selling, penalized heavily by the largest negative coefficient in the model; and the lowest book-to-market (0.091), suggesting the strongest premium valuation relative to fundamentals. The arithmetic of these penalties exceeds the boost from CEG's strong earnings beat, producing a net negative composite α and the Sell recommendation.

How Part 1 informed the approach

Three findings from Part 1 directly shaped the composite construction: the multivariate t-stats determined which signals to use, the implied Sharpe ratios determined the relative emphasis (momentum at SR 4.4 carries the heaviest weight), and the fraction-positive analysis confirmed that the surviving signals are stable enough to warrant out-of-sample application — momentum and SUE were positive in 90% of the sixty months.

03.5

Discretionary analysis.

~250 words · Part 2B(4) · stress-test

The model is purely backward-looking and quantitative. It misses three large forward-looking shifts that materially affect the recommendation.

For Constellation Energy (CEG) and Vistra Corp (VST): the model entirely misses the unprecedented surge in U.S. electricity demand from artificial-intelligence training and hyperscale data centers. Both firms operate large unregulated nuclear fleets with capacity attributes — 24/7, carbon-free baseload — that hyperscalers are actively contracting at substantial premium pricing. The quantitative model recommends Sell on CEG and Neutral on VST based on relative momentum, volatility, and a Q1 earnings miss for VST. A discretionary fund would interpret VST's earnings miss as backward-looking noise relative to the long-duration value of nuclear-baseload PPAs being signed at 2x to 3x historical rates. CEG is signing similar deals. On a discretionary view, both names could be generational buys; the quant model penalizes them precisely on the metrics that matter least to the new demand thesis.

For NextEra Energy (NEE): the model ignores interest-rate sensitivity, which is decisive for capital-intensive renewable developers. Higher-for-longer rates compress project IRRs, and NEE's accelerated capex pipeline relies on continued access to debt at moderate spreads. The "Buy" recommendation is implicitly conditional on a stable or declining rate environment that the signals cannot detect.

Discretionary funds with access to alternative data (data-center contract pipeline, private capex disclosures, management commentary) would diverge sharply from the quant call here. A pure quant fund would short VST and long NEE; a hybrid fund would likely hold both VST and CEG as nuclear-AI-thesis longs and trim NEE on rate concerns. The two recommendations are not reconcilable from data alone — the difference is which time horizon and which information set the strategy is built on.

03.6

Disclosed limitations.

Known unknowns
Limitation 01

Forward-looking blind spots

The model is backward-looking by construction. Discontinuities — regulatory changes, technology cycles, M&A — are invisible until they affect prices. This is why discretionary stress-testing matters.

Limitation 02

Forced ranking on small N

With N = 3 to 10 names, relative ordering does the work. Statistical significance at the firm level is weak. The framework is best understood as a relative-value lens, not a standalone signal.

Limitation 03

Signal definitions

Live signals must be calculated consistently with how they were defined in the training panel. Where definitions differ — for instance, using EPS surprise / consensus as a SUE proxy — the deviation must be disclosed and justified.

Limitation 04

Panel-period dependence

FM coefficients reflect the regime captured in the training panel. Out-of-sample regimes — rate environments, monetary policy, market structure shifts — can attenuate or invert relationships. Re-estimation is required for any live deployment.
